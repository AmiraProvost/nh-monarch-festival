Our Story

About the NH Monarch Festival

Fourteen years of celebrating the monarch butterfly — and the people, plants, and places that make their survival possible. Here’s the story behind our favorite day of the year.

How It All Started

From a Love of Monarchs
to a Community Tradition

Donna Miller has been a monarch enthusiast since the early 2000s when her son was in second grade and his class studied the butterfly. She was hooked! Donna began learning everything she could. She became a citizen scientist for Monarch Watch in 2008, tagging monarchs annually before their fall migration.

In 2012, Donna and her husband, Jim, held the first monarch festival at Petals in the Pines in Canterbury, New Hampshire, to celebrate this amazing creature. The first festival “happened to be when the monarch’s overwintering population was at about its lowest ever,” Donna says. The numbers were so low that year — not just in New Hampshire, but across North America — that there were no monarchs at the festival.

However, that did not dissuade the Millers from continuing the festival tradition. What began as a small gathering of butterfly enthusiasts has now grown into one of the region’s most beloved annual conservation celebrations.

In 2022, Amira Provost joined the Millers in educating the public with conservation efforts and providing information about citizen science projects, such as the Monarch Larva Monitoring Project (MLMP) through Monarch Joint Venture. That same year, the Millers saw the biggest crowd ever — over 300 attendees!

As the event grew over the years, parking at Petals in the Pines became a huge issue. The Millers were worried someone might get hurt on the road coming to the event. They decided that the 2023 and 2024 NH Monarch Festivals would be broken down into a few small-scale events spanning several days. The new structure was effective, but sadly created more work.

After the 2024 festival, the Millers handed the management of the festival to Amira Provost. The following season, the festival returned as a one-day event and was held at the beautiful and historic Canterbury Shaker Village. However, while Petals in the Pines was too small of a venue to hold the one-day festival, it was discovered Shaker Village was too large. The new event coordinator is hoping that Belmont Village is within the “Goldilocks” zone of being just the right size.

“We’ve also seen the monarchs rebound in the last 10 years, some years better than others. But there is still a long way to go to get their population into a safety zone of an average of 6 hectares for their overwintering area.”
— Donna Miller, NH Monarch Festival Founder

Our Mission

Conservation Through Connection

The NH Monarch Festival exists to educate, inspire, and mobilize our community around monarch butterfly conservation. We believe that hands-on learning, joyful celebration, and local connection are among the most powerful conservation tools we have.

Every festival activity — from tagging demonstrations to milkweed planting to kids’ crafts — is designed to create a lasting relationship between our community and the natural world. We want every person who visits to leave with knowledge, inspiration, and a concrete next step they can take at home.

🏻

Education

Sharing the science of monarch migration, biology, and conservation with visitors of all ages through interactive demonstrations and expert-led presentations.

🌿

Habitat

Encouraging the planting of native milkweed and nectar plants, and supporting efforts to restore monarch habitat across New Hampshire and beyond.

🤝

Community

Building a network of monarch advocates — backyard gardeners, teachers, students, artists, and neighbors — united by a shared love of the natural world.

A Fourteen-Year Journey

Festival Milestones

From its humble beginnings to a beloved regional tradition, every year has brought new growth — in attendance, programming, and community impact.

2013 — Year One

The First Festival

A small gathering of monarch enthusiasts in Belmont plants the seeds of what would become an annual tradition. The first festival featured a butterfly release, a handful of local vendors, and more passion than polish.

2015 — Year Three

Monarch Tagging Begins

The festival partners with Monarch Watch to introduce live monarch tagging demonstrations — one of the first public tagging events in New Hampshire. Attendance doubles from year one.

2018 — Year Six

Kids’ Programming Expands

A dedicated children’s activity area is introduced, featuring crafts, games, and guided nature walks. The festival begins drawing families from across Belknap County.

2021 — Year Nine

Conservation Partnerships Grow

The festival deepens its ties with regional conservation organizations, featuring educational booths from NH Audubon, the NH Division of Forests & Lands, and native plant nurseries.

2024 — Year Thirteen

Best-Attended Festival Yet

The 13th Annual NH Monarch Festival draws its largest crowd ever, with expanded vendor programming, live music, and a monarch waystation dedication ceremony.

2026 — Year Fourteen

Here We Come

The 14th Annual NH Monarch Festival returns on August 22nd. New activities, beloved traditions, and the same heart at the center: bringing people and monarchs together.

What We Believe

Our Values

The NH Monarch Festival is guided by a set of values that show up in everything we do — from the vendors we welcome to the activities we create.

🌿

Rooted in Conservation

Everything we do is in service of monarch butterfly survival. Conservation isn’t a backdrop — it’s the whole point.

👐

Open to Everyone

The festival is designed to be joyful, accessible, and welcoming to all ages, backgrounds, and levels of nature knowledge.

🏡

Proudly Local

We celebrate New Hampshire’s natural heritage, feature local vendors and makers, and strengthen the bonds of our own community.

✨

Joyful & Celebratory

We believe that joy is one of the most powerful forces for change. We celebrate the monarch — and celebrate each other — with genuine delight.

📚

Learning at Every Age

From toddlers to grandparents, every visitor should leave knowing something they didn’t know before — and feeling the better for it.

🌎

Think Local, Act Global

Monarchs migrate across a continent. Every milkweed plant, every waystation, every child who learns to love them matters on a global scale.
